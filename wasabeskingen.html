Little common sense about wasabi

When you taste our Wasabi, you might find yourself reminiscing about Japan, a land often referred to as the "green gold." Many people associate the consumption of delicious seafood with the accompaniment of wasabi, believing that it provides similar benefits. However, in reality, many wasabi products on the market are not entirely natural; they are often made from a substance called "horseradish." Horseradish, compared to genuine wasabi, has a lower economic value and is relatively cheaper. Therefore, while these products may offer some degree of spiciness, they cannot fully replace the unique flavor and nutritional value of authentic wasabi. True wasabi is a plant that grows in cold, high-altitude environments, requiring a special habitat for cultivation. Its distinctive flavor and spiciness have evolved over time in adaptation to the high mountain surroundings, making it challenging to replicate with other ingredients. Hence, if you seek the most authentic wasabi experience, it is still recommended to choose genuine wasabi products. In summary, while substitutes on the market can provide a certain level of flavor, they cannot fully substitute for the unique taste and value brought by genuine wasabi. Wasabi, often hailed as "green gold," remains a distinctive and precious ingredient that adds a lot of charm to Japanese cuisine. We hope you can savor the uniqueness of this beauty and gain an understanding of what makes wasabi so special. Thank you for choosing to taste our Wasabi, and we hope you can experience its exclusive allure that sets it apart from other products.

Growth environment

Because its leaves resemble those of the Malvaceae family and grow on high mountains, it is called "wasabi". If it is cultivated at high altitude, it needs to be grown in a cool, cool place. In addition, it needs to maintain an environment with flowing water to grow well.

shape

Native to China and Japan, it is known as "Eutrema tenue". It is a cruciferous herbaceous plant. Its underground rhizome is slender and strong, with traces of fallen petioles. The leaves are clustered and the long leaves are heart-shaped.
